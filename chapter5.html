Chapter Summary: Management of Innovation

1. The Concept and Nature of Innovation

Innovation is the transformation of an idea, through creativity, into products, processes, and services. It is the systematic application of creative ideas to explore market opportunities and gain a competitive advantage. According to Peter Drucker, "The enterprise that does not innovate inevitably ages and declines."

The nature of innovation is categorized into four main forms:

  • Radical (Breakthrough) Innovation: A transformative process that solves a problem in a unique way, disrupting the status quo (e.g., the yam pounder in Nigeria in the mid-70s). It is riskier and more expensive.
  • Disruptive (Transformational) Innovation: Creates a new market and value network, eventually displacing established market-leading firms (e.g., mobile phones disrupting wired telephones in Nigeria in 2002). This concept was coined by Clayton Christensen in 1995.
  • Incremental Innovation: Involves minor improvements to existing technologies. Common in catch-up countries like Nigeria to find low-risk opportunities for business growth.
  • Eco-Innovation: Focuses on reducing environmental risk, pollution, and resource waste while bringing economic benefits. It is divided into Internal (Eco-product, Eco-process, Eco-organisational) and External activities.

2. Types of Innovation (OECD, 2005)

The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development identifies four distinct types of innovations:

  • Product Innovation: Introduction of a good or service that is new or significantly improved in its characteristics or uses (e.g., ABS braking in cars, breathable fabrics).
  • Process Innovation: Implementation of a new or significantly improved production or delivery method (e.g., GPS tracking, RFID tracking, motorised cassava graters, automatic garri roasters).
  • Marketing Innovation: Implementation of a new marketing method involving significant changes in product design, packaging, placement, promotion, or pricing (e.g., first use of franchising, celebrity endorsements).
  • Organisational Innovation: Implementation of a new organisational method in the firm’s business practices, workplace organisation, or external relations (e.g., supply chain management systems, build-to-order production).

3. Theories of Innovation

Several theoretical perspectives explain how innovation occurs:

  • Linear Model (Technology-Push): Suggests that advances in science/R&D directly determine the rate and direction of innovation.
  • Demand-Pull Model: Holds that market demand for products stimulates inventive activity more than scientific advances do.
  • Schumpeter's Theory of Innovation: Joseph Schumpeter viewed the entrepreneur as the catalyst of "creative destruction." He identified three stages: Invention (first demonstration), Innovation (first commercial application), and Diffusion (spreading throughout the market). He famously distinguished an inventor (who discovers) from an innovator (who applies the discovery commercially).
  • Evolutionary & Induced Approaches: Evolutionary stresses slow, incremental change, while Induced emphasizes that changes in relative prices drive technical change (e.g., expensive labour leads to labour-saving technology).

4. National System of Innovation (NSI) in Nigeria

The NSI is a network of institutions whose interactions determine the innovative performance of national firms. The main activity in the NSI is learning. In Nigeria, the key actors are:

  1. Education/Research (Knowledge Actor): Universities, polytechnics, and research institutes (e.g., FIIRO, PRODA) responsible for R&D and manpower development.
  2. Production/Industrial Actor: The manufacturing firms and SMEs. Organised centrally by the Manufacturers Association of Nigeria (MAN).
  3. Finance/Funding Actor: Provides venture capital and loans. Includes schemes like the Small and Medium Industries Equity Investment Scheme (SMIEIS), where banks set aside 10% of Profit Before Tax for SMEs.
  4. Government/Public Policy Actor: Ministries (e.g., Science and Technology) that formulate policies, regulate activities, and lay down national priority programmes.

5. Innovation Financing and Change Management

R&D funding in Sub-Saharan Africa is historically poor. Nigeria's Gross Domestic Expenditure on R&D (GERD) has averaged just 0.2% of its GDP since 2007.
Furthermore, to implement technical changes successfully, organizations must practice effective Change Management. As Mohammad (2015) states, "All innovations result in change... but not all change is innovation."

Points to Note & Exam Hints

  • Inventor vs. Innovator: Remember Schumpeter’s distinction. An inventor discovers new methods/materials; an innovator applies those discoveries to make economic performance.
  • Disruptive Innovation: If you see "Clayton Christensen (1995)", it refers to Disruptive Innovation. Example: Mobile phones replacing landlines.
  • Schumpeter's 3 Stages: Invention ➔ Innovation ➔ Diffusion (which follows an S-shaped curve).
  • SMIEIS Funding: Remember that banks were required to set aside 10% of their Profit Before Tax as equity contribution for SME development.
  • R&D Expenditure: Sub-Saharan African countries spend less than 1% of their GDP on R&D, and Nigeria averages about 0.2%.
  • Cassava Processing Examples: A motorised grater, automatic garri roaster, and hydraulic press are all examples of Process Innovation.

Mnemonics to Note

1. Nature of Innovation: "R.I.D.E."

  • Radical (Breakthrough, transformative)
  • Incremental (Minor changes, low risk)
  • Disruptive (Creates new market, displaces old)
  • Eco-Innovation (Reduces environmental impact)

2. Types of Innovation (OECD): "P.P.M.O."

  • Product (New goods/services)
  • Process (New production/delivery methods)
  • Marketing (New design, packaging, pricing)
  • Organisational (New business practices/workplace structures)

3. Schumpeter’s 3 Stages of Innovation: "I.I.D."

  • Invention (First demonstration)
  • Innovation (First commercial application)
  • Diffusion (Spreading through the market)

4. Actors in the National System of Innovation: "K.I.F.G."

  • Knowledge/Education Actor (Universities, Research Institutes)
  • Industry/Production Actor (MAN, SMEs)
  • Finance Actor (SMIEIS, BOI, Venture Capital)
  • Government Actor (Ministries, Policymakers)
