Chapter Summary: Feasibility Study and Business Plan

1. The Concept of a Feasibility Study

A feasibility study is a crucial analysis carried out to determine the workability and profitability of a business idea before any resources are invested. It aims to justify the project in terms of business viability, technical feasibility, and cost-effectiveness. A feasibility report is the formally documented output of this study.

Key Reasons for a Feasibility Study: To assess practicability, identify logistic problems, attract investors/banks, determine execution steps, focus the project team, and evaluate alternative business models.

2. Stages of a Feasibility Study

Conducting a feasibility study follows a strict decision-making process. If any stage is unfavourable, the study is terminated:

  1. Market Study: Ascertains future total demand, competitive situation, projected sales, and selling price. If favourable, proceed to the next stage.
  2. Technical Analysis: Establishes if the project is technically feasible. Analyzes location, production processes, machinery, factory layout, availability of inputs, and estimates fixed/start-up costs.
  3. Financial Analysis: Converts market and technical data into projected financial statements. Determines commercial profitability, cash flows, return on investment (ROI), return on equity (ROE), and breakeven volume.
  4. Management: Selects the appropriate form of business organization, internal structure, and staffing requirements.

3. Structure of a Feasibility Report

A standard feasibility report generally follows this structure:

  • Executive Summary: Brief overview, financing, demand/supply, and conclusion.
  • Chapter 1 (Introduction/Background): Name of firm, location (access to infrastructure), project description, and objectives.
  • Chapter 2 (Market Feasibility): Market description, demand/supply trends, competitive position, projected sales, and economic contributions (taxes, labor).
  • Chapter 3 (Technical Feasibility): Products, processing activities, firm capacity, machinery (e.g., the CD-6 machine in the Gbolajoko Saw Mill example), layout, raw materials, utilities, waste disposal, and labor.
  • Chapter 4 (Financial/Economic Study): Financial assumptions, total project cost, projections (income, cash flow, balance sheets), and profitability analysis (NPV, IRR, PBP).
  • Chapter 5 (Conclusion and Recommendations)

4. The Concept of a Business Plan

A business plan is a written document that outlines the future activity, strategy, and operations of a proposed venture. Crucial Difference: A business plan is developed only after a feasibility study has established that the business idea is viable. Its length varies, but accuracy and content matter more than page count.

Importance: It conveys vision to potential investors, helps attract key employees/suppliers, provides direction, structures thinking, prompts future planning (e.g., preparing for competitors), and identifies weaknesses.

5. Types of Business Plans

Business plans can be divided into four distinct types:

  • The Miniplan: A short plan (1-10 pages) used to quickly test a concept or measure interest from a minor investor. It is a prelude, not a substitute for a full plan.
  • The Working Plan: An internal tool used to operate the business. It is long on detail but short on presentation (omits fancy binders, uses workplace jargon).
  • The Presentation Plan: Designed for outsiders (bankers, investors). Uses standard business vocabulary, high-quality printing, graphics, and must be completely accurate and internally consistent.
  • The Electronic Plan: Used for computer-driven presentations or for investors who want to deeply analyze complex spreadsheets.

6. Elements of a Business Plan

There are three primary parts: Business Concept, Market Place, and Financial Section. These are broken down into seven key components:

  • Executive Summary: A concise one-page highlight of the mission, market, funding, and forecasts.
  • Business Description: Contains the Mission Statement (purposes, needs addressed, guiding principles), Business Overview, and Product/Service description.
  • Market Strategies & Competitive Analysis: Details target customer profiles (demographics, psychographics) and competitor strengths/weaknesses.
  • Marketing Plan: Focuses on four areas: Products, Pricing, Distribution (channels of delivery), and Promotion.
  • Operating Plan: Details production methods, facilities, equipment, operations, and Legal Issues (IP protection like patents/trademarks, and regulatory compliance).
  • Financial Plan: Forecasted income, cash flows, balance sheets, and breakeven analysis (usually projecting 2 to 5 years).

Points to Note & Exam Hints

  • Feasibility vs. Business Plan: A feasibility study tests if an idea will work (conducted first). A business plan outlines how to execute and manage the business (conducted second).
  • Feasibility Stages: Remember the order—Market, Technical, Financial, and Management. If Market is bad, the study stops. If Market is good but Technical is bad, it stops.
  • Types of Plans: Be able to differentiate them. Working plan = internal use/jargon allowed. Presentation plan = external use/high quality/no jargon.
  • Marketing Plan Elements: Always addresses four areas: Product, Price, Distribution, Promotion.
  • Case Study Context: The textbook uses the "Gbolajoko Saw Milling Factory, Oyo" as its sample feasibility report, utilizing equipment like a Barn saw engine, CD-6 machine, and hydraulic press.
