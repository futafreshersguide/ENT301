Chapter Summary: Theories of Growth

1. The Concept and Determinants of Business Growth

New opportunities are critical to the success of a business, whether in manufacturing or services. The primary goal of business expansion is to bring in new sources of revenue, though strategic reasons like developing partnerships, distribution channels, and new technology also play a part. A business's growth can be measured using four key determinants: an increase in sales volume, an increase in output, an increase in capital employed, and an increase in productive capability.

However, many organisations fail to achieve their growth targets due to two major reasons: inadequate consideration of opportunities (within or adjacent to the core business) and inadequate organisational infrastructure that cannot support successful execution.

2. Reasons and Challenges of Growth

Reasons for Expansion:

  • Capturing economies of scale and scope: Increasing production lowers the average cost of each unit produced.
  • Reducing costs: Larger companies can significantly reduce unit costs.
  • Market leadership & Influence: Achieving brand leadership, setting industry standards, and gaining power and survivability.
  • Accommodating customers & Retaining talent: Growing to meet key customer needs and generating promotion opportunities to retain expert employees.
  • Greater Profitability: Servicing more customers with reduced costs leads to better profit levels.

Challenges of Expansion:

  • Keeping control: The entrepreneur must let some control go, build a strong organisational structure, and delegate decision-making.
  • Changing business methods: Growth requires more discipline and professionalism in everyday dealings.
  • Reporting and Hiring: Growth brings outside stakeholders who expect regular performance reports and budgets. Furthermore, knowing who to hire and when is a massive challenge for start-ups.
  • Cash flow management: Poor cash flow management breaks more businesses than fierce competitors.

3. Internal Growth Strategies

Internal growth relies on developing the business using internal resources. This includes New Product Development, improving existing products, increasing market penetration, extending product lines, and geographic expansion.

Diversification is a major internal strategy used when current markets are saturated. There are four types of diversification:

  1. Horizontal: Offering new products/services to the current customer base (e.g., a cheese company adding a new variety of cheese).
  2. Vertical: Moving back or forward in the production cycle (e.g., a house reconstruction company selling construction materials).
  3. Concentric: Introducing new products that utilise the prevailing technologies or marketing systems (e.g., a bread bakery starting to produce biscuits).
  4. Conglomerate: Diversifying into a totally unrelated business in both marketing and technology (e.g., a footwear company merging with a pharmaceutical company).

Pros of Internal Growth: Maximum control, preserves culture, incremental pace.
Cons of Internal Growth: Slow form of growth, requires developing new resources, and difficult to recoup failed investments.

4. External Growth Strategies

External strategies rely on establishing relationships with third parties.

  • Mergers vs. Acquisitions: A Merger is the combination of two companies to form a new one (Company A + Company B = Company C). An Acquisition (Takeover) is when one firm buys another, making it a subsidiary within its portfolio (Company A + Company B = Company A).
  • Types of Mergers: Horizontal (similar products), Vertical (different stages of production), Conglomerate (unrelated activities), and Concentric (adjacent industries).
  • Licensing & Franchising: Licensing grants permission to use intellectual property (patents, trademarks) for a fee. Franchising licenses a trademark and the method of doing business (e.g., McDonald's, Mr. Biggs) for an ongoing royalty.
  • Strategic Alliances & Joint Ventures: Alliances are informal partnerships to achieve a specific goal without creating a new entity. A Joint Venture pools resources to create a separate, jointly owned organisation (e.g., Sony Ericsson).

5. Buy-In and Buy-Out Strategies

These involve the purchase of an existing company by a management team.

  • Management Buy-Out (MBO): The existing management of the company takes a stake and purchases the business.
  • Management Buy-In (MBI): A completely new, outside team takes on the ownership and management of the business. This is seen as riskier by funders due to the learning curve.
  • Leveraged Buyout (LBO): The buyout is funded with significant amounts of borrowed money (debt), using the target company's assets as collateral.
  • BIMBO (Buy-In Management Buy-Out): A combination strategy where both existing and incoming management members join to create a more rounded team.

Points to Note & Exam Hints

  • Merger vs. Acquisition Formula: The classic exam trick! Merger = A + B = C. Acquisition = A + B = A.
  • Concentric vs. Conglomerate Diversification: Concentric uses existing technologies (a bakery making biscuits). Conglomerate enters a totally unrelated industry.
  • LBO Risk: A Leveraged Buyout is high-risk, high-reward because the company must generate high cash flows immediately to pay the interest on the massive debt used to buy it.
  • MBO vs MBI: MBO = *Inside* management buys it. MBI = *Outside* management buys it.
  • Joint Venture vs. Alliance: A Joint Venture creates a brand new, separate company (like Sony + Ericsson = Sony Ericsson). A Strategic Alliance does not create a new entity.

Mnemonics to Note

1. Types of Diversification: "H.V.C.C."

  • Horizontal (Same customers, new products)
  • Vertical (Different production stage)
  • Concentric (Similar technology/marketing)
  • Conglomerate (Totally unrelated)

2. Types of Buy-Outs/Buy-Ins: "M.M.L.B."

  • Management Buy-Out (MBO - Insiders)
  • Management Buy-In (MBI - Outsiders)
  • Leveraged Buy-Out (LBO - High Debt)
  • BIMBO (Buy-In Management Buy-Out - Combination)
