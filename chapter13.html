Chapter Summary: Marketing

1. The Concept of Marketing and Selling

Marketing is essentially about managing customer relationships profitably. According to Peter Drucker, it is the process of creating value for customers and building strong relationships to capture value in return. The Chartered Institute of Marketing (CIM) defines it as the management process of anticipatorily identifying and satisfying customer requirements profitably.

Marketing vs. Selling:

  • Marketing: Focuses on creating "product pull" (demand). It stresses the buyer's needs, aims for long-term customer loyalty, starts before production, and focuses on earning profit through maximum customer satisfaction.
  • Selling: Focuses on "product push". It stresses the seller's needs, takes a short-term approach to maximize sales volume, and starts only after production is completed.

2. Functions & Basics of Marketing

To successfully deliver products to consumers, nine core marketing functions must be accomplished: Buying, Selling, Financing, Storage (crucial for perishables), Transportation, Processing, Risk Taking (e.g., insurance), Market Information, and Grading/Standardisation.

The 5 Basics of Marketing for Small Businesses:

  1. Understand the Customer: Successfully meet their actual needs.
  2. Create Value: Develop a competitive advantage (e.g., better features, location, or credit terms).
  3. Communicate Value: Use Word of Mouth (WOM) and publicity to make a great first impression.
  4. Make it Easy to Buy: Set convenient hours, offer credit, and deliver timely.
  5. Create a Long-Term Relationship: Turn first-time buyers into loyal, repeat customers.

3. The Marketing Mix (The 4Ps)

Discovered by Neil Bordon and popularised by Jerome E. McCarthy in the 1960s, the marketing mix consists of controllable variables used to achieve objectives in the target market.

  • Product: Anything offered to satisfy a want/need.
    - Consumer Goods include Convenience (staples, impulse, emergency), Shopping (furniture), Specialty (Rolex watches), and Unsought goods (caskets, life insurance).
    - Industrial Goods include Materials/parts, Capital items, and Supplies/services.
    *Product decisions involve Attributes, Branding (trademarks, copyrights), Packaging (Primary, Secondary, Shipping), Labelling, and Support Services.*
  • Price: The economic value of a product.
    - Strategies: Price Skimming (setting an initial high price) and Price Penetration (setting an initial low price to quickly gain market share).
    - Methods: Discounts, Allowances, List price, Premium pricing, Mark-up, and Cost-plus pricing.
  • Place (Distribution): The movement of goods from producer to consumer via intermediaries (Retailers, Wholesalers, Distributors, Agents). Channels range from Zero-level (Direct to consumer) to Three-level (Producer → Agent → Wholesaler → Retailer → Consumer).
  • Promotion: Informing and persuading consumers. The promotional mix includes: Advertising (paid/non-personal), Sales Promotion (short-term incentives like coupons), Personal Selling (direct contact, best for technical goods), Publicity (unpaid news coverage, highly credible), and Direct Marketing.

4. The Extended Marketing Mix & E-Marketing

To accommodate the modern service-based economy, Booms and Bitner (1981) added three more variables to make it the 7Ps:

  • People: Employees, management, and customer service interactions.
  • Process: The flow of activities leading to an exchange of value.
  • Physical Evidence: Sensory experiences like prestigious offices, staff uniforms, and business cards.

Electronic Marketing (E-Marketing): Uses digital channels (SEO, social media, blogs) to achieve marketing goals. Unlike Traditional Marketing (print, broadcast) which is expensive, time-consuming, and offers no audience interaction, E-Marketing is highly interactive, measurable, cheap, easily adjustable, and offers 24/7 global exposure.

Points to Note & Exam Hints

  • Product Push vs. Product Pull: A classic exam question! Selling relies on Product Push. Marketing relies on Product Pull.
  • Types of Packaging: Remember the toothpaste example! The tube is the Primary package, the cardboard box is the Secondary package, and the large carton used for transport is the Shipping (Tertiary) package.
  • Price Skimming vs. Penetration: Skimming = High initial price (best when customers aren't price-sensitive). Penetration = Low initial price (best for highly price-sensitive markets).
  • Marketing Mix Origins: Remember that Neil Bordon discovered the 4Ps, Jerome E. McCarthy popularised them, and Booms & Bitner added the extra 3Ps in 1981.
  • Publicity vs. Advertising: Publicity is unpaid and has higher credibility because it comes from independent third parties (like news stations). Advertising is paid space.

  • Convenience goods (frequent, easy purchases)
  • Shopping goods (compared carefully, like used cars)
  • Specialty goods (unique effort, like a Lamborghini)
  • Unsought goods (unknown/unwanted, like cemetery plots)

  • Personal Selling
  • Advertising
  • Publicity
  • Sales Promotion
  • Direct Marketing
