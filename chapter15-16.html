Chapter Summary: Chapter 15 - New Opportunities for Expansion

1. History of Commerce in Nigeria

Commercial activities in Nigeria evolved through several distinct phases:

  • Subsistence & Barter: Early Nigerians produced only for their families before adopting "trade by barter" to meet wider needs.
  • Trans-Saharan Trade: Camels and caravan routes linked Nigerian traders (in Kano, Borno, Zaria, Katsina) with North African merchants, exchanging kolanuts and gold for salt and cowries.
  • European Contact (15th Century): Missionaries and traders brought weapons and cloth in exchange for palm oil and slaves via coastal towns like Badagry, Bonny, and Calabar.
  • Colonial Era & 1960s: The abolition of the slave trade led to the establishment of the United African Company (UAC). By the 1960s, Nigeria became a major exporter of palm oil, cocoa, groundnut, and later, crude oil.
  • Modern Era: The introduction of Information and Communication Technologies (ICTs) has birthed e-Commerce, revolutionising modern trade.

2. Electronic Commerce (e-Commerce) & Its Tools

e-Commerce is the ability to perform transactions involving the exchange of goods or services between parties using electronic tools and techniques. It provides fast, cost-efficient, and time-saving business communication compared to traditional postal, telex, or fax services.

Tools of ICT include: Desktop computers, laptops, palmtops, smartphones, and the Internet.

3. Types of e-Commerce Transactions

There are four main types of e-commerce:

  1. Business to Business (B2B): Interactions between two business entities (e.g., a steel industry website matching suppliers and buyers). It streamlines operations and fulfilling needs.
  2. Business to Consumers (B2C): Businesses selling directly to consumers online (e.g., Amazon, Rediff). It reduces intermediaries, allows 24/7 sales, and enables "just-in-time" inventory delivery.
  3. Consumer to Consumer (C2C): Consumers selling directly to other consumers, acting as the cyber version of traditional auction houses (e.g., eBay, Baazi.com).
  4. Consumer to Business (C2B): Empowers the customer to dictate the terms. The consumer acts as the "offeror" setting a price, and the business is the "offeree" (e.g., Priceline.com for airline/hotel bookings).

4. Advantages and Disadvantages of e-Commerce

  • Advantages: Ultimate shopping convenience, allows SMEs to compete globally with large corporations, reduces operating/labor costs, eliminates middlemen (lowering prices), and eliminates the risk of carrying physical cash.
  • Disadvantages: Subjects local businesses to fierce global competition, excludes people without ICT access or computer literacy, may cause local unemployment, limits physical inspection of goods before purchase, and can be highly capital-intensive to set up.

5. Starting an e-Commerce Company

An entrepreneur must decide between In-house operations (buying servers, hardware, and hiring IT staff) and Out-sourcing (hiring web developers and using third-party Internet Service Providers).

E-commerce relies heavily on two components that must be seamlessly integrated:

  • Front-end Operations: What the customer interacts with (e.g., website functionality, search capabilities, shopping cart, secure payments).
  • Back-end Operations: Behind-the-scenes processes (e.g., accounting, finance, inventory management, order fulfillment, shipping).

Key Website Features: Search tools, Shopping carts (to calculate totals), Secure servers (encryption for safety), Merchant accounts (for credit cards), and e-mail response systems. To establish legitimacy, businesses use certification authorities like VeriSign.

Chapter Summary: Chapter 16 - Managing Transition from Start-up to Growth

1. Change vs. Transition

A business, like a living organism, must grow. Change is an observable event that occurs quickly (how things will be different). Transition is the intangible, unobservable psychological process of moving people and managing resources through phases to make that change work.

2. Greiner's Six Phases of Business Growth and Crises

As businesses grow, they pass through specific phases, each culminating in a specific crisis that must be managed to reach the next phase:

  1. Phase 1: Start-Up/Infancy (Growth through Creativity) → Ends in a Leadership Crisis. The founder's informal style no longer works, requiring a professional manager.
  2. Phase 2: Fast Growth/Childhood (Growth through Direction) → Ends in an Autonomy Crisis. The business becomes too complex for one manager to control, requiring delegation.
  3. Phase 3: Adolescent (Growth through Delegation) → Ends in a Control Crisis. Middle managers make sub-optimal decisions, causing top executives to lose control.
  4. Phase 4: Maturity (Growth through Coordination and Monitoring) → Ends in a Red Tape Crisis. Reorganizing into product groups creates stifling bureaucracy and antagonism between layers.
  5. Phase 5: Growth through Collaboration → Ends in an Internal Growth Crisis. Bureaucracy is replaced by trust and matrix management, but internal systems eventually become overstretched.
  6. Phase 6: Growth through Extra-organisational Solutions → Solving internal limits by pursuing external alliances, mergers, and acquisitions.

3. Critical Factors of a Successful Transition Process

  • 1. Planning: Setting objectives and strategies. Involves 8 steps (analyzing environment, setting objectives, forecasting, determining alternatives, evaluating, selecting, formulating support plans, budgeting). Pitfalls to avoid: non-collaborative attitude, losing control, ignoring stakeholders.
  • 2. Decision-Making: Selecting a course of action. Can be Programmed (routine/standardized rules) or Non-programmed (unique/no rules). Decisions are made under conditions of Certainty (perfect info), Risk (assigning probabilities), or Uncertainty (no info).
  • 3. Control: Evaluating and correcting activities. Can be Internal (managing quality, waste) or External (NUC accreditation, ICAN auditors). A "Tall" organization has many managers with a narrow span of control; a "Flat" organization has few managers with a wide span of control.
  • 4. Personal Discipline: Doing what needs to be done, when it needs to be done, whether you like it or not. Requires patience, motivation, and determination.
  • 5. Stress and Pressure Management: While some pressure is good (stimulates alertness), pressure overload causes Stress (physical/mental health issues like ulcers, high blood pressure, depression). The 5 causes of stress (CIPD) are: workload, management style, relationships, organisational change, and lack of support.
    Solution: Use the READ technique (Relax, Exercise, Attitude, Diet).

Points to Note & Exam Hints

  • Types of e-Commerce: Differentiate them clearly! B2B (Business to Business), B2C (Amazon), C2C (Auction sites/eBay), C2B (Priceline/Customer names the price).
  • Front-end vs. Back-end: Front-end is what the customer sees (shopping cart, website design). Back-end is behind the scenes (inventory, shipping, accounting).
  • The Crises of Growth: Exam questions love pairing the growth phase with its resulting crisis! Creativity leads to Leadership Crisis. Direction leads to Autonomy Crisis. Delegation leads to Control Crisis. Coordination leads to Red Tape Crisis.
  • Decision Making Conditions: Certainty = exact outcomes known. Risk = probabilities can be assigned. Uncertainty = no information at all.
  • Programmed vs. Non-programmed Decisions: Programmed decisions are routine (like granting employee leave). Non-programmed are unique and rare (like preparing for a massive corporate merger).
