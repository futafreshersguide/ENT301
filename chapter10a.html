Chapter Summary: Concept of Business and New Venture Creation

1. The Four Stages of a Business Venture

A business venture is a start-up enterprise formed with the expectation of financial gain. A business generally goes through four distinct stages (Liepner, Jordy & Schultz, 1991):

  1. Conceptual Stage (6 months - 2 years): The focused entrepreneurial activity begins here. It involves a four-step process:
    a. Recognising Opportunity (finding where problems exist).
    b. Analysing the Idea (gathering information to see if it makes economic sense).
    c. Organising Resources (combining the 4 Ms: man, money, materials, machines).
    d. Mobilising Resources (preparing for day-to-day running, e.g., renting office space).
  2. Start-up Stage (1.5 - 2 years): The entrepreneur's vision is tested. The business plan goes through many adjustments (products, marketing, financing).
  3. Stabilisation Stage (1 - 2 years): Cash flow stabilises, monthly financial statements may show a profit, and the organisational structure takes shape.
  4. Growth Stage: The business moves beyond stabilisation, requiring the help of professionals and specialists. Long-term strategies are developed for expansion into new markets.

2. Requirements to Start a Business

According to Burns (2004), starting a business requires more than just an idea. Setting up is triggered by Push factors (unemployment, disagreements with management, having no alternatives) and Pull factors (independence, achievement, personal wealth). The five key requirements are:

  • The Idea: Must be linked to market demand or fill a gap. Innovation here can mean invention, doing things better/cheaper, new technology, or new delivery methods.
  • Personal Attributes: The owner must possess necessary skills, stamina, commitment, and tolerance for risk.
  • Customers: There must be a 'need', and the world must know about the product and be persuaded to buy it.
  • Competitors: Requires detailed knowledge of the market structure and existing competition.
  • Launch a Strategy: Utilizing Porter’s (1985) three generic strategies for competitive advantage:
    - Low Price/Cost Leadership: Selling at or below industry average to gain market share.
    - Differentiation: Offering unique attributes valued by customers.
    - Focus: Concentrating on a narrow segment to achieve either cost advantage or differentiation.

3. The Business Plan

A business plan is a written document outlining the future activity, strategy, and operations of a proposed venture. Its key roles include: setting objectives, ensuring profitable delivery, raising finance, helping control the business, identifying weaknesses, and communicating with stakeholders.
Key contents include: Executive summary, Market analysis, Product uniqueness, Competition, Idea protection (patents), Management team, Marketing strategy, Production/operations, Financial projections, Funding requirements, and Exit strategy.

4. Forms of Business Ownership & Legal Issues

When starting, entrepreneurs must consider tax, personal liability, control, capital requirements, and continuity. The major forms are:

  • Sole Proprietorship: One-man business. Easy to form, flexible, all profits go to the owner. However, it suffers from unlimited liability, limited capital, and lack of continuity.
  • Partnership: An association of 2 to 20 persons. Formed with a Deed of Partnership. Types of partners include:
    - Active/Managing: Runs day-to-day operations.
    - Sleeping/Dormant: Contributes capital and shares profits, but does not manage.
    - Nominal: Lends their name to the firm, invests no capital, takes no profit, but is liable to third parties.
    - Limited: Losses are restricted to their share; no management role.
  • Corporations (Limited Liability Company): Governed by the Companies and Allied Matters Act (CAMA) 1990. They are separate legal entities with perpetual succession.
    - Private Limited (Ltd): 2 to 50 members. Prohibits public subscription to shares. Restricts share transfers.
    - Public Limited (Plc): Minimum 7 members, no maximum. Allows public subscription and free transfer of shares. Requires a Certificate of Trading to commence business.
    *Formation requires filing the Memorandum of Association, Articles of Association, and Statement of Nominal Capital.*
  • Cooperative Society: A business owned and controlled by those who use its services (minimum 10 members, no maximum). Managed democratically. Its primary motive is service to members, not profit maximization. Examples: Producers', Consumers', Credit and Thrift, Agricultural, Housing, and Marketing cooperatives.

Points to Note & Exam Hints

  • Memorandum vs. Articles of Association: The Memorandum governs external relations (name, registered office, objectives, authorized capital). The Articles govern internal management (duties of directors, rights of shareholders, meeting rules).
  • Certificate of Trading: A Private company can start business immediately after receiving a Certificate of Incorporation. A Public company (Plc) must wait to receive a Certificate of Trading.
  • Nominal Partners: Remember that a nominal partner does not invest money or share profits, but they are liable to third parties because they lend their name to the business!
  • Porter's Generic Strategies: Remember the big three: Cost Leadership, Differentiation, and Focus.
  • Membership Limits:
    - Partnership: 2 to 20.
    - Private Ltd: 2 to 50.
    - Public Plc: 7 to infinity.
    - Cooperative: 10 to infinity.
