Chapter Summary: Sources of Funds

1. Introduction to Business Finance

Business financing involves the sources and means of raising funds for business enterprises. Decisions regarding how much finance is required, where, when, and how long it is needed are critical. Obtaining funds is especially difficult for micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) because they are considered riskier than established businesses. Sources of funds can be categorized by duration (short-term, medium-term, long-term) or by origin (internal vs. external).

2. Internal Sources of Finance

These are funds generated from within the organization. They include:

  • Owners' Investment: Personal savings used as start-up or additional capital. It is the most reliable source for sole proprietorships and carries no interest.
  • Retained Profits/Earnings: Profits ploughed back into the business instead of being shared as dividends. Only available to firms trading for over a year.
  • Sale of Stock: Selling unsold stock to raise quick capital and reduce holding costs.
  • Disposal of Fixed Assets: Selling old or underused assets (e.g., old vehicles) to raise working capital.
  • Debt Collection: Collecting cash on overdue accounts (from debtors) quickly to enhance liquidity.

3. External Sources of Finance

These are funds generated outside the business. They include:

  • Venture Capital (VC): Private equity provided to high-growth, technology-driven emerging businesses. VCs take an equity stake, play an active role, use other people's money, and aim to exit via an IPO or merger.
  • Angel Financing: High net worth individuals (business angels) who invest their personal money into new businesses with less stringent conditions.
  • Bank Overdraft: A short-term facility allowing a current account holder to withdraw beyond their balance up to a limit. Excellent for seasonal fluctuations.
  • Bank Loan: A formal long-term arrangement with interest and collateral.
  • Leasing: A medium-term arrangement to use an asset without outright purchase. Includes Operating Lease (short-term, cancelable, lessor maintains), Financial Lease / Net Leasing (medium/long-term, lessee maintains, non-cancelable), and Sale and Leaseback (selling an asset and leasing it back).
  • Hire Purchase: Paying an initial deposit and the balance in installments. The business owns the asset after the final payment.
  • Factoring: Selling debts/accounts receivable to a factor agent (bank/finance company) at a discount for immediate cash.
  • Trade Credit: 'Buy now, pay later' arrangement based on trust.
  • Crowdfunding: Raising small amounts from a large number of people via the internet.

4. Other Long-Term Sources (Shares & Bonds)

  • Common Stock (Equity Shares): Represents true ownership (risk capital). Holders bear the highest risk, reap the highest rewards, and have voting rights.
  • Preference Shares: An intermediate position. Holders get fixed dividends before equity holders but usually have no voting rights. Types include Cumulative (arrears are paid later), Non-cumulative, Participating, Redeemable, and Convertible.
  • Debenture Stocks: Corporate bonds secured by assets representing a firm's indebtedness. Holders are creditors who get fixed interest regardless of profit.

5. Informal & Semi-Formal Finance in Nigeria

  • Informal Finance: Voluntary associations with minimal paperwork. The most common is the Esusu (ROSCAs). Known as ajo (Yoruba), isusu/otutu (Igbo), osusu (Edo), efe (Ibibio), oku (Ijaw), and adashi (Hausa). Money Lenders are also informal but highly exploitative.
  • Semi-Formal Finance: More organized than esusu. Includes Cooperative Thrift Societies (encourages saving) and Cooperative Credit Societies (provides low-interest loans to members).

6. Government Initiatives in Funding

The Nigerian government has established several funds to support SMEs:

  • MSMEDF (N220 Billion): Launched in Aug 2013 (Pres. Goodluck Jonathan). 9% interest. 60% reserved for women, 10% for start-ups, 2% for persons with disabilities.
  • Anchor Borrowers' Programme (ABP): Launched in Nov 2015 (Pres. Buhari). Links smallholder farmers and large processors. N40 billion set aside from MSMEDF.
  • Commercial Agricultural Credit Scheme (CACS): Established in April 2010. Financed by N200 billion DMO bonds. Renewed until 2025.
  • YouWin: Launched in 2011 (Pres. Goodluck). N50 billion seed fund for innovative youth job creation.
  • National Directorate of Employment (NDE): Established in 1986 (Pres. Babangida) to create jobs for graduates.
  • NERFUND: Established in 1989 (Pres. Babangida) to provide medium/long-term finance for SMEs.
  • Bank of Industry (BOI): Established in Oct 2001 (Pres. Obasanjo). Formed by the merger of NIDB (1964), NBCI (1973), and NERFUND (1989).

Points to Note & Exam Hints

  • Venture Capital vs. Angels: A classic exam question! Business Angels invest their own personal money. Venture Capitalists invest other people's money (institutional funds).
  • The BOI Merger: Memorize the three organizations that merged in 2001 to form the Bank of Industry: NIDB (1964), NBCI (1973), and NERFUND (1989).
  • Leasing Differences: Operating Lease = lessor maintains, cancelable. Financial Lease = lessee maintains, non-cancelable without paying the remaining balance.
  • MSMEDF Percentages: Remember that 60% is reserved for women, 10% for start-ups, and 2% for persons with disabilities.
  • Esusu Local Names: Match the name to the tribe! Ajo/Esusu (Yoruba), Adashi (Hausa), Otutu/Isusu (Igbo).
