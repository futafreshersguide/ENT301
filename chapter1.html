Chapter Summary: Entrepreneurship and Intrapreneurship

1. The Concept of Entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurship involves identifying investment opportunities, pooling resources, and forming an enterprise while taking risks to maximize gains. The term was first coined in the 18th century by Richard Cantillon, derived from the French word "enterprendre" (to undertake). Various scholars view it differently: Joseph Schumpeter defines it as "innovation" that results in new demand, while the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) defines it as transforming a business concept into a new venture with high growth potential.

2. Who is an Entrepreneur?

An entrepreneur is someone who perceives a market opportunity, motivates others, and mobilizes resources to meet it. They are risk-takers and innovators. Key characteristics of a successful entrepreneur include:

  • Self-confidence: Optimistic, independent, and having a positive mental attitude.
  • Risk Taking: Willing to face and take up challenges.
  • Task-result Oriented: Driven by a need for achievement, profit-oriented, and hardworking.
  • Leadership and Managerial Skill: Gets along well with others, listens to suggestions, and guides teams.
  • Integrity: Honest and highly committed to standards.
  • Future-oriented & Originality: Innovative, resourceful, flexible, and having strong foresight.

3. The Roles of Entrepreneurs

  • Visionary/Innovative Roles: Discovering and launching new ideas into business operations.
  • Social Roles: Redistributing wealth, providing employment, and transforming traditional industries into modern enterprises.
  • Economic Roles: Bearing uncertainty, mobilizing savings, and utilizing local raw materials.
  • Technological Roles: Adapting traditional technology to modern systems and developing local technological competence.
  • Managerial Roles: Managing daily operations, securing funding, and marketing products.

4. Types of Entrepreneurs

  • Innovative: Creators of newer, better, and more economical ideas (e.g., Ratan Tata).
  • Imitating (Copy cats): Observe existing systems and replicate them in a better manner without taking the initial innovation risk.
  • Fabian: Very careful, cautious, lazy, and shy away from innovations unless their survival is threatened.
  • Drone: Die-hard conservatives who resist change, stick to orthodox methods, and would rather suffer business losses than adapt.
  • Social: Focus on poverty alleviation and social transformation with the zeal of a business entrepreneur.

5. Reasons for Entrepreneurship

  • Push Factors: Unpleasant events that force someone into business (e.g., unemployment, job dissatisfaction, relocation).
  • Pull Factors: Favorable attractions (e.g., profitability, independence, enabling environment).
  • Supportive Factors: Culture, social condition, peer groups, and working experience.

6. Corporate Entrepreneurship (Intrapreneurship)

Intrapreneurship is entrepreneurship within an already established organization. An intrapreneur is an employee given the freedom to explore new opportunities using the company's existing resources. It is encouraged by a dynamic external environment, supportive management activities, and systems that reward creative efforts while emphasizing individual accountability.

7. Importance of Entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurship drives economic development by increasing the National Income and GDP (SMEs contribute about 37% to Nigeria's GDP). It creates massive employment opportunities (SMEs make up 85% of firms in Nigeria), improves the standard of living through innovative goods, reduces rural-urban drift, and fosters local technology development.

8. Theories of Entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurship is explained through five major theoretical lenses:

  • A. Economic Theory: Focuses on market functions and economic factors.
    - Richard Cantillon: Viewed the entrepreneur as an arbitrager who buys at a certain price to sell at an uncertain price, bearing risk.
    - Jean-Baptiste Say: Emphasized the managerial role of uniting land, labor, and capital.
    - Joseph Schumpeter: Focused on innovation and "creative destruction," identifying 5 sources of innovation (new goods, new methods, new markets, new raw materials, new organization).
    - Frank Knight: Differentiated between insurable risk and uninsurable true uncertainty.
    - Israel Kirzner: Stressed the entrepreneur's alertness to discover and exploit profit opportunities in the market.
  • B. Psychological Theory: David McClelland propounded the Achievement Motivation Theory, arguing that the inner "need for achievement" drives entrepreneurial activity.
  • C. Sociological Theory: Suggests the environment shapes entrepreneurs. Thomas Cochran focused on cultural upbringing, while Frank Young focused on inter-group relationships.
  • D. Opportunity-based Theory: Peter Drucker stated that entrepreneurs do not cause change, but rather search for change, respond to it, and exploit it as an opportunity.
  • E. Resource-based Theory: Barney highlighted that access to Financial capital, Social networks, and Human capital (education/experience) predicts an individual's ability to discover and act upon opportunities.
