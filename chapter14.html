Chapter Summary: Ethics and Social Responsibility

1. The Concept of Ethics and Business Ethics

Ethics refers to the "study of whatever that is right and good for humans." Originating from the Greek word ethos (meaning 'custom and usage'), it involves codes of values and principles that govern what is right versus what is wrong. Behaving ethically includes having concern for others, respecting autonomy, trustworthiness, compliance with the law, fairness, and doing no harm.

Business Ethics is the study of behavior and morals in a business situation. It involves applying principles of honesty and fairness to relationships with co-workers and customers. Business ethics set the standard for how business is conducted.
Why it matters: Ethical behavior builds customer loyalty, retains good employees, creates a positive work environment, and helps avoid legal problems. To enforce this, governments globally protect Whistle blowers—individuals who report illegal activity within a firm to the press, government, or outside parties.

2. Ethical Institutions in Nigeria

To promote ethical behavior and combat corruption, the Federal Government of Nigeria established several key agencies:

  • Economic and Financial Crimes Commission (EFCC): Set up in 2003. It investigates financial crimes (money laundering, advance fee fraud, contract scams), coordinates anti-crime laws, and traces or freezes terrorist and crime proceeds.
  • Independent Corrupt Practices Commission (ICPC): Established in 2000. It receives and investigates complaints from the public on corrupt practices, supervises the review of systems that aid corruption, and educates the public against bribery.
  • National Agency for Food and Drug Administration and Control (NAFDAC): The Council was formed in 1992, but the Agency was created by Decree No 15 of 1993 (edited in 1999). It controls the unethical behavior of food and drug manufacturers to prevent deaths from fake drugs and bad food.
  • Standard Organization of Nigeria (SON): Created in 1971 (currently operating under SON Act No 14, 2015). It is in charge of standardization in Nigeria, ensuring quality materials are used (e.g., for buildings and roads) to prevent the distribution of fake and substandard products.

3. Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

Origin: CSR originated in the 1950s as American corporations rapidly increased in size and power, and gained momentum in the 1960s and 1970s to combat social problems like poverty, pollution, and unemployment. In 1960, Keith Davis introduced the Iron Law of Responsibility, which stated that the social responsibility of businessmen needs to be proportionate to their social influence.

CSR is the approach an organization takes in balancing its responsibilities toward various stakeholders (Owners, Workers, Suppliers, Customers, and Communities). It is also known as corporate accountability, responsible entrepreneurship, corporate citizenship, or the "Triple Bottom Line."

4. Types of Social Responsibilities

A firm's social responsibilities are categorized into four distinct types:

  1. Economic Responsibility: The basic function of the firm to produce goods and services and sell them at a profit.
  2. Legal Responsibility: The constraint to operate strictly within the laws defined by the state (e.g., contracts, financial regulations).
  3. Ethical/Cultural Responsibilities: Operating within a framework of cultural norms and unwritten rules about what to do and what not to do.
  4. Discretionary Responsibilities: Voluntary responsibilities that the entrepreneur accepts even though they are not generally expected (e.g., exceptional worker conditions, environmental philanthropy).

The Nexus: CSR cannot exist in an unethical ecosystem. Business ethics provide the moral compass that allows an organization to behave responsibly in society.

Points to Note & Exam Hints

  • Establishment Dates: Memorize the dates for the Nigerian agencies! SON = 1971. NAFDAC = 1993. ICPC = 2000. EFCC = 2003.
  • The Whistle Blower: Know the definition. A whistle blower is an insider who reports illegal activity to outside parties (government, press).
  • The Iron Law of Responsibility: Coined by Davis in 1960. It states that a business's social responsibility must be exactly proportionate to its social influence and power.
  • The Four Types of Responsibility: Be able to differentiate them. Economic = Profit. Legal = Law. Ethical = Cultural norms. Discretionary = Voluntary/Extra good deeds.
  • EFCC vs. ICPC: EFCC generally handles heavy financial crimes, money laundering, and cyber fraud. ICPC handles public complaints regarding bribery and corrupt practices in public/private systems.
