Chapter Summary: Social Entrepreneurship in Nigeria

1. Meaning of Social Entrepreneurship

Social entrepreneurship is an alternative business model that produces social and environmental value to address social problems. The term is generally grouped into three schools of thought:

  1. A not-for-profit initiative using management schemes to create social value.
  2. The socially responsible practice of commercial businesses engaged in cross-sector partnerships.
  3. A means to alleviate social problems and catalyse social transformation.

Social Entrepreneurs are change agents who use earned-income strategies to pursue social objectives. They seek both a financial and a social return on investment. They act boldly, without being limited by resources, to create solutions that the state welfare system cannot or will not meet.

2. Social Enterprise vs. Business Entrepreneurship

A social enterprise is a revenue-generating venture with a supreme focus on social objectives. Unlike a traditional non-profit, it does not rely solely on government or donor support. The earned income is reinvested to advance the mission rather than shared as profit with shareholders. It pursues a double bottom line (social and financial) or a triple bottom line (social, financial, and environmental).

  • Business Entrepreneurship: Starts a venture for profit, measures success by wealth created, relies heavily on material resources, operates in stable/risk-free environments, and follows a single bottom line (profit).
  • Social Entrepreneurship: Starts a venture for social change, measures success by social good provided, relies on non-material resources (passion, faith), operates in difficult/risky/low-profit environments, and follows a double or triple bottom line.

3. Sources of Finance for Social Enterprises

Social enterprises can access funds through three main categories:

  • External Options:
    - Conventional Finance (Equity, Friends/Family, Banks/Credit Unions, Credit Cards).
    - Social Investors (Socially Responsible Investing [SRI] and "patient capital", avoiding "bads" like weapons or tobacco).
    - Philanthropic Grants (Gifts from foundations or wealthy individuals).
    - Government and Donor Capital (Government contracts and international funding).
  • Internal Options: Managing cash flow through Accounts Receivable (A/R) and Accounts Payable (A/P).
  • Crowd funding: Using the Internet for collective cooperation, networking, and pooling money to support efforts.

4. Youth Social Entrepreneurship

This engages young people in bringing positive change to society, moving them away from the idea of "charity" to something that is lasting and socially impactful.
Characteristics of Youth Entrepreneurs: They are dreamers, sensitive to societal needs, change makers, act boldly without being limited by finances, create self-generated revenue streams (not charity), and do not wait for opportunities but create them.
Nurturing them: Can be done through education/mentoring, networking, financial support, and engaging the media to promote their efforts.

5. Non-Governmental Organisations (NGOs) & Religious Organisations

NGOs are non-profit, voluntary citizens' groups. Their three major functions are service delivery, educational provision, and public policy advocacy. They promote empowerment ("the ability to make choices and changes").

Types of NGOs by Orientation:

  • Charitable: Directed towards meeting the needs of the poor.
  • Service: Provision of health, family planning, or education.
  • Participatory: Characterised by self-help projects where local people contribute cash, tools, or labour.
  • Empowering: Helps poor people understand their potential power to control their own lives.

Types of NGOs by Level of Operation: Community-based Organisations (CBOs), City-wide Organisations, and National/International NGOs.
*NGOs promote Sustainable Community Development, which has 5 dimensions: (1) Local economic diversity, (2) Self-reliance, (3) Energy reduction/recycling, (4) Biological diversity protection, (5) Social justice.*

Religious Organisations: Focus on spiritual empowerment, driven by the belief that empowerment helps people rediscover their God-given potentials, reflecting God's intention for equality and dignity.

Points to Note & Exam Hints

  • The Bottom Line Difference: Expect questions comparing Business vs. Social Entrepreneurship. Remember that Business = Single bottom line (profit). Social = Double (social/financial) or Triple bottom line (social/financial/environmental).
  • Socially Responsible Investing (SRI): This type of investment avoids "bads" (companies selling weapons, alcohol, tobacco) and encourages human rights and fair trade.
  • NGO Orientations: Make sure you can differentiate between Charitable, Service, Participatory, and Empowering orientations. Participatory involves the locals providing labor/tools, while Empowering focuses on awareness and control over their lives.
  • 5 Dimensions of Sustainable Community Development: Economic diversity, self-reliance, energy reduction, biological diversity, and social justice.
  • Youth Entrepreneurship: A key characteristic is that they do not follow the charity approach; they create self-generated revenue streams.

Mnemonics to Note

1. Schools of Thought on Social Entrepreneurship: "N.S.A."

  • Not-for-profit initiative to create social value
  • Socially responsible practice by commercial businesses
  • Alleviate social problems and catalyze transformation

2. Types of NGO Orientations: "C.S.P.E."

  • Charitable
  • Service
  • Participatory
  • Empowering

3. Sources of External Finance: "C.S.P.G."

  • Conventional Finance (Banks, equity, credit cards)
  • Social Investors (SRI, patient capital)
  • Philanthropic Grants (Foundations)
  • Government and Donor Capital (Contracts)
