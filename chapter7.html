Chapter Summary: Women Entrepreneurship

1. Concept and Nature of Women Entrepreneurship

Women Entrepreneurs are defined as women or a group of women who initiate, organise, and operate a business enterprise. Despite making up over 50% of the world's population, women own and manage significantly fewer businesses than men. The UK Global Entrepreneurship Report notes that men are about two and a half times more likely to be entrepreneurs than women.

Women-owned SMEs represent about 31 to 38% of formal SMEs in emerging markets. Often, women start businesses in retail, education, and service industries, which are sometimes perceived as less critical to economic growth than high-tech industries. However, women entrepreneurship is increasingly recognized as an untapped source of economic growth and job creation.

2. Factors Influencing Women into Business

Women are motivated to start businesses by two main groups of factors:

  • Push Factors (Negative Forces): These force women into entrepreneurship for survival. Examples include job/career frustration, the need for a flexible schedule to balance family obligations, redundancy, unemployment, the death/sickness of a husband, or insufficient family income. Research shows that the vast majority (about 4/5ths) of women are driven by push factors.
  • Pull Factors (Positive Forces): These are attractions that draw women into business. Examples include government support, the desire for independence and self-fulfilment, improving financial positions, commercialising a hobby, and the influence of role models. Only about one-fifth of women are drawn by pull factors.

3. Characteristics of Women Entrepreneurs

Successful women entrepreneurs exhibit specific attributes:

  • Imaginative: They use original ideas and a well-planned approach to identify opportunities.
  • Attribute to Work Hard: They possess the stamina and diligence needed to build an enterprise.
  • Persistence: They have the intention and tenacity to fulfill their dreams.
  • Ability and Desire to Take Risk: They are proficient in planning, making estimates, and taking calculated risks.
  • Profit Earning Capacity: They have the capacity to maximize returns on invested capital.

4. Women's Role in Economic Development

The advancement of women is closely tied to the steady economic development of a nation. Their specific roles include: Job openings provision, the growth of savings (related to working capital), increasing purchasing power, increasing business volume, improving the standard of living, and solving community-related problems.

5. Barriers to Women Entrepreneurship

Women face unique socio-economic obstacles that limit their access to opportunities:

  1. Lack of Self-Confidence: The socialization process throughout women's lives may negatively affect their self-assessments regarding firm creation.
  2. Lack of Relevant Networks: Women tend to rely heavily on informal networks (like family) and often lack the time or access for formal business networking.
  3. Lack of Role Models: Historically, there are fewer visible female entrepreneurs to emulate.
  4. Inequality of Access to Credit: Women often struggle to secure finance because they lack the required collateral.
  5. Lack of Experience (Human Capital): They may lack the specific knowledge and skills needed to discover and exploit opportunities.
  6. Socio-Cultural Barriers: Multiple family roles limit risk-taking. Also, women in developing countries tend to spend their income on the household (food/education) rather than reinvesting in business out of fear of failure.
  7. Lack of Support Services: Heavy bureaucracy, complex business plan requirements, and poor infrastructure (like transportation and power supplies).
  8. Work/Home Conflict: The pressure of childcare and gender-role expectations.

6. Supports for Women Entrepreneurs

To enhance women's entrepreneurship, governments and societies must provide: affordable childcare to increase labour force participation, strong local and international business networks, periodical evaluation of SME policies, systematic skills upgrading, special loans, enterprise centres, and targeted advisory support.

Points to Note & Exam Hints

  • The 1/5th Rule: Remember that only one-fifth (1/5) of women are drawn into entrepreneurship by Pull factors. The rest are forced by Push factors.
  • Male vs. Female Ratio: Men are 2.5 times more likely to be entrepreneurs than women.
  • SME Representation: Women-owned SMEs represent 31 to 38% of formal SMEs in emerging markets.
  • Human Capital Theory: Posits that individuals with higher quality human capital (knowledge, skills, experience) achieve higher performance in executing tasks (Becker, 1964).
  • Push vs. Pull: Exam questions love testing the difference between these two. Push = survival/negative reasons (loss of job, bad pay). Pull = ambition/positive reasons (independence, hobby, government grants).
