Chapter Summary: Business Opportunity Evaluation

1. Business Idea vs. Business Opportunity

The ability to recognise business opportunities distinguishes entrepreneurs from non-entrepreneurs. It is crucial to understand the difference between an idea and an opportunity:

  • Business Idea: The base of the pyramid. It is a perceived and conscious thought of a business (usually centered on a product/service exchanged for money) that exists in an abstract form in the creator's mind. Not all ideas are profitable.
  • Business Opportunity: A business idea that has been researched upon, refined, and packaged into a promising venture ready to be launched. It is a proven concept that generates ongoing income.

2. Features & Sources of a Good Business Idea

A promising business idea must possess five key features: Relevant (solves a problem/fulfils a need), Innovative (uses modern methods), Unique (distinct from competitors), Acceptability (accepted by a large market), and Profitable in the long run.

Entrepreneurs can source new ideas from:

  1. Family members and friends (social networks).
  2. Discovery of loopholes/gaps in the market.
  3. Prior experience in an industry.
  4. Personal skills, hobbies, and interests.
  5. Market research/environmental scanning.
  6. Media (newspapers, internet, journals).
  7. Government policies/regulations (e.g., a tax cut creating a market for tax-sensitive products).
  8. Customers (complaints/comments).
  9. Business associates.
  10. Distribution channels (wholesalers/retailers who know end-user needs).

3. Venture Capitalists vs. Business Angels

Both are sources of private equity for unlisted, high-growth companies, but they differ in the source of their funds:

  • Business Angels: Individuals (often successful business people) who commit their personal funds into rewarding business concerns.
  • Venture Capitalists (VCs): Firms that use other people's money (e.g., through an Initial Public Offer - IPO) to invest.

4. Investment Decision Criteria

To reach a GO/NO-GO decision, VCs and Angels use specific criteria to evaluate ventures. They look for:

  • Entrepreneur/Managerial Team: Financial stake/commitment, honesty, determination, and a balanced, complementary team.
  • Product/Service: Proprietary protection (patents), uniqueness, technical edge, and a working prototype.
  • The Market: Demonstrated market acceptance, significant growth rate, projected market share, and little threat of competition in the first three years.
  • Financial Characteristics: A healthy Return on Investment (ROI), a high expected rate of return (e.g., a return equal to at least 10 times the investment within 5 years), and clear equity percentages.
  • Continuity of the Firm: Long-term implications and liquidity (the possibility of the firm going public or being acquired).

5. Project Evaluation & Capital Budgeting Techniques

Capital budgeting is the decision of a firm to invest its current funds most efficiently in long-term assets in anticipation of future benefits. Small businesses must properly evaluate projects to avoid over-investing or under-investing. Evaluation techniques are divided into two categories:

A. Traditional / Non-Discounted Cash Flow Techniques:

  • Pay Back Period (PBP): The number of years it takes to recover the initial investment.
    Formula: Cash Outlay / Annual Cash Inflow.
    Rule: Accept if the calculated PBP is less than management's predetermined time.
  • Accounting Rate of Return (ARR): The ratio of average return to average investment.
    Formula: Average annual profit (after tax) / Average investment.
    Rule: Accept if ARR is higher than the predetermined ARR. Choose the higher ARR for mutually exclusive projects.

B. Modern / Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Techniques (Considers the time value of money):

  • Net Present Value (NPV): The surplus of the present value (PV) of cash receipts over the PV of cash outlay.
    Rule: Accept if NPV is positive (NPV > 0). Reject if negative.
  • Internal Rate of Return (IRR): The discount rate that equates the NPV to exactly zero (calculated via trial and error / interpolation).
    Rule: Accept if IRR > k (firm's cost of capital).
  • Profitability Index (PI): The ratio of the PV of cash inflows to the initial cash outflow/capital invested.
    Formula: PV of cash inflows / Capital Invested.
    Rule: Accept if PI ≥ 1. Reject if PI < 1.

Points to Note & Exam Hints

  • Idea vs. Opportunity: An idea is just a thought; an opportunity is an idea that is researched, packaged, and ready to launch.
  • Angel vs. VC Funding: Always remember: Angels use their own money; Venture Capitalists use other people's money.
  • NPV Advantages: NPV is considered superior because it recognises the time value of money, considers all cash flows over the project's entire life, and is consistent with the objective of maximising shareholder wealth.
  • The 10x Return Rule: In the financial characteristics criteria, VCs generally look forward to a capital gain equal to at least 10 times the investment within 5 years.
  • Decision Rules to Memorise:
    - NPV must be Greater than 0.
    - PI must be Greater than or equal to 1.
    - IRR must be Greater than the cost of capital (k).

Mnemonics to Note

1. Features of a Good Business Idea: "P.A.I.R. U."

  • Profitable in the long run
  • Acceptability (large market)
  • Innovative
  • Relevant
  • Unique

2. Venture Capitalist Evaluation Criteria: "C.E.M.P. F."

  • Continuity of the firm (liquidity, going public)
  • Entrepreneur/Managerial Team
  • Market (acceptance, growth rate)
  • Product/Service (proprietary, prototype)
  • Financial characteristics (10x ROI)

3. Project Evaluation Techniques: "T.M." (Traditional vs Modern)

  • Traditional: PBP (Pay Back Period) & ARR (Accounting Rate of Return)
  • Modern (DCF): NPV (Net Present Value), IRR (Internal Rate of Return), PI (Profitability Index)
