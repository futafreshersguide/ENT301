Chapter Summary: Technological Entrepreneurship

1. Defining Technological Entrepreneurship

In a knowledge-based economy, technological entrepreneurship is crucial for wealth creation and national competitiveness. It is distinct from commercial entrepreneurship:

  • Technological Entrepreneurship: Involves product manufacture or the provision of technical services, utilizing scientific breakthroughs to commercialize innovative products. Engineers and scientists are uniquely qualified for this.
  • Commercial Entrepreneurship: Involves trading, buying and selling, or the provision of non-technical services.

2. The Industrial Revolution

Taking place between the late 18th and early 19th centuries in Britain, the Industrial Revolution was a fundamental event that birthed modern technology. It was categorized by three major shifts:

  1. Introduction of machines to replace human labor.
  2. Use of inanimate sources of power instead of animate sources.
  3. Introduction of new and abundant raw materials.

Factors that Aided the Revolution: The ingenuity of British farmers (e.g., Jethro Tull’s mechanical seeder), a highly developed banking/credit market system, a regulated free market, and a labor surplus.
Key Inventions: The textile industry led the way (James Hargreaves' cotton spinning jenny in 1765). The steam engine, originally developed by Thomas Savery and Thomas Newcomen, was significantly improved by James Watt in the 1760s (using coal).
Impact: Increased productivity and living standards, but also brought "mixed blessings" like population booms and negative social spillover effects.

3. Intellectual Property (IP) in Tech Entrepreneurship

Tech entrepreneurs must protect their knowledge (ideas, processes, know-how) to sustain a competitive edge. IP sources include:

  • Patents: Lasts for 20 years. Applies to machines, chemicals, and processes. The basic types are Utility, Plant, and Design patents.
  • Copyright: Gives moral and financial exclusive rights over books, software, films, and music.
  • Trademark/Service Mark: A symbol or name that distinguishes a product. Other types include certification and collective marks. (Agencies like NAFDAC and SON handle quality certification in Nigeria).
  • Trade Secrets: The oldest form of protection. Suitable for know-how and software. Requires no formal registration and lasts as long as the secret is kept.

4. Agencies Supporting Technological Entrepreneurship in Nigeria

The Federal Government of Nigeria supports tech entrepreneurship through several key agencies:

  • Raw Materials Research and Development Council (RMRDC): Established by Decree 39 of 1987 (commenced in 1988). Formulates policies for the innovative exploitation of local raw materials.
  • Technology Incubation Centres (TIC): Helps entrepreneurs overcome start-up hurdles. The Lagos TIC at Agege (1993) was the first. There are currently 21 TICs in Nigeria, supervised by the Federal Ministry of Science and Technology under Decree 5 of 1995. (There are two types: Industrial business incubators and Technology business incubators).
  • Federal Institute of Industrial Research, Oshodi (FIIRO): Established in 1956 to carry out industrial research in textiles, food processing (e.g., cassava flour for bread), and prototype fabrication.
  • National Office for Technology Acquisition and Promotion (NOTAP): Registers and evaluates technology transfer agreements and promotes intellectual property rights.
  • Other Institutes: NIMR (Yaba, Lagos), Leather Research Institute (Zaria), NIFOR (Benin), and National Root Crops Research Institute (Umudike).

5. Policy Recommendations for Nigeria

To maximize tech entrepreneurship, Nigeria must: Provide infrastructural facilities to reduce production costs, encourage the patronage of locally made goods, strengthen research institutes and SME institutions, search for entrepreneurs to commercialize university research, and establish technology parks linked to knowledge centers.

Points to Note & Exam Hints

  • Tech vs. Commercial: Expect questions asking you to differentiate the two. Remember: Tech = Manufacturing/Technical services; Commercial = Trading/Buying & Selling.
  • Historical Dates & Inventors:
    • Jethro Tull: Horse-drawn hoe & mechanical seeder.
    • James Hargreaves: Cotton spinning jenny (1765).
    • James Watt: Improved the coal-fired steam engine (1760s).
  • Agency Establishment Dates: FIIRO was established in 1956. RMRDC was established by Decree 39 in 1987. The first TIC was established in Lagos (Agege) in 1993.
  • Trade Secrets: Always remember that Trade Secrets are the oldest form of IP protection and require no registration formalities.
