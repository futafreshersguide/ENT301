Chapter Summary: Creativity & Intellectual Property Rights

1. The Concept of Creativity and Intellectual Property (IP)

Creativity refers to the invention of new things that have value—bringing something into existence that did not exist before, whether as a product, process, or thought. Intellectual Property (IP) refers to the legal rights resulting from intellectual activity in the industrial, scientific, literary, and artistic fields. IP laws safeguard creators by granting them exclusive monopoly rights to exploit and benefit from their creations, limited in scope, duration, and geographical extent. IP is traditionally divided into two branches: Industrial Property (patents, trademarks, designs) and Copyright.

2. Origin and Administration of IP in Nigeria

IP in Nigeria traces back to the colonial era when the English Trademark Ordinance was introduced before the 1914 amalgamation. Today, IP is administered by specific bodies:

  • Trademarks, Patents, and Designs: Governed by the Trademarks Act (1965) and Patents and Designs Act (1970). They are administered by the Commercial Law Department (Trademarks, Patents and Designs Registry) of the Federal Ministry of Commerce and Industry.
  • Copyright: Governed by the Copyright Act (1988) and administered by the Nigerian Copyright Commission (NCC), an agency under the Federal Ministry of Justice.

Internationally, Nigeria is bound by the World Trade Organisation’s TRIPS Agreement (1994) which establishes minimum standards for IP protection.

3. Forms of Intellectual Property Protection

A. Patent
A patent is a legal document granting an inventor the right to exclude others from making, using, selling, or importing an invention for a specified period (generally 20 years from the filing date).
Qualifications for a Patent (Three Tests):

  1. Novel: The invention must be new and not previously exist or be disclosed.
  2. Non-obvious (Inventive Step): It must be a significant improvement to existing technology, not just a simple change.
  3. Useful (Industrially Applicable): It must have a practical purpose and not be for illegal/immoral use.

Application Process: The text notes that applications require three parts: a Specification (general description), Claims (detailed statements of how it works), and Drawings (illustrations).

B. Trademarks
A distinctive sign or symbol that distinguishes a company's goods and services from competitors. They can be words, letters, numerals, devices, or 3D signs.
Duration: Registered for 7 to 20 years and can be renewed indefinitely (though protection is lost if unused for 5 consecutive years).
Types of Trademarks: Service Marks (for services like airlines), Collective Marks (owned by an association for its members), and Certification Marks (certifies compliance with a defined set of standards; not owned by an association).

C. Trade Secrets
Confidential business information (e.g., recipes, formulas, customer lists, business plans). There is no formal registration for trade secrets. To be legally protected, the information must: (1) not be in the public domain, (2) have economic value based on its secrecy, and (3) be protected by reasonable measures taken by the owner.

D. Copyright
Deals with the rights of intellectual creators. It protects the form of expression of ideas (choice of words, notes, colors), not the ideas themselves. Because Nigeria is a member of the Berne Convention, copyright protection is automatic upon creation (formality-free) as long as it is fixed in a tangible medium and original.
Categories of Work: Literary, Artistic, Cinematograph Films, Sound Recordings, and Broadcasts.
Duration of Copyright in Nigeria:

  • Literary, musical, or artistic works (excluding photos): 70 years after the author's death.
  • Corporate body/Government works: 70 years after first publication.
  • Cinematograph films, photographs, and broadcasts: 50 years after creation/first broadcast.

Fair Use: Allows the use of copyrighted works without permission for purposes like criticism, comment, teaching, and reporting.

E. Royalty (Payment)
A payment made to a property owner for the use of their property (analogous to rent), such as paying a composer a percentage of proceeds for performing their musical work.

Points to Note & Exam Hints

  • Patent Disputes: In Nigeria, if multiple people apply for a patent for the same invention, the patent goes to the person who first invented the item (decided via an "interference" proceeding). In most other countries, it goes to the "first person to file."
  • Copyright Registration: Copyright is automatic in Nigeria; there is no mandatory registration. However, the NCC operates a "Copyright Notification scheme" to maintain a data bank of authors.
  • Limits on Copyright: Court opinions, acts of Congress, and government documents are not protected by copyright and can be reproduced freely.
  • Transfers of Copyright: An assignment transfers actual property ownership rights to a third party, whereas a license simply authorizes a third party to use the work while the original author retains ownership.
  • Enforcement: Under the TRIPS Agreement and WIPO, enforcement is crucial. In Nigeria, copyright inspectors have powers analogous to police officers to investigate infringements.

Mnemonics to Note

1. Tests to Qualify for a Patent: "N.N.U."

  • Novel (Must be new)
  • Non-obvious (Significant improvement, inventive step)
  • Useful (Industrially applicable, practical purpose)

2. Parts of a Patent Application: "S.C.D."

  • Specification (General description)
  • Claims (Detailed statements of how it works)
  • Drawings (Illustrations)

3. Types of Trademarks: "S.C.C."

  • Service Marks (For services like hotels/airlines)
  • Collective Marks (Owned by an association)
  • Certification Marks (Based on quality standards/compliance)

4. Eligible Works for Copyright: "L.A.C.S.B."

  • Literary Works (Books, software, poems)
  • Artistic Works (Paintings, architecture, maps)
  • Cinematograph Films (Movies, visual sequences)
  • Sound Recordings (Audio fixations)
  • Broadcasts (Television, radio by wire or satellite)
